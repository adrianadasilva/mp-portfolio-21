Design Evidence

Colour Theory

To choose the colours for the website, I looked into colour psychology. As the subject of my photography portfolio was nature, I wanted to illustrate that in the website design. For this, I decided to choose green, as it is a colour that represents nature and growth. It also symbolizes healing and restfulness.

To use the colours I had picked on the website, I used hexadecimal codes. This is a 16-symbol code that represents a value, with the first two standing for red, the second for green and the third for blue (Fisher, 2021). In my case, I used #6B6F41 for my primary colour and #BDC667 for my secondary. The primary colour is the one used to catch the user’s attention, while the secondary highlights elements that do not need immediate attention.

For the primary and secondary colours of the website, I decided to pick a monochromatic colour scheme. This scheme is based on one single tint or colour and any of the other colours are different shades or tints of the main one. Though shades, tints and tones are often confused as the same, a tint is a mixture of the colour with white, where a shade is a mixture of the colour with black. By both tinting and shading we produce a new tone.

Fisher, T. (2021) What Is Hexadecimal? [Blog] Lifewire, Available at: https://www.lifewire.com/what-is-hexadecimal-2625897 [Accessed 10 April 2021]

Typography

For my website, I sourced the fonts from Google Fonts. It is a service that hosts fonts and it means designers can pick the font they want, copy the code snippets and drop it into their code. Google Fonts has nearly 1,000 different font families, is free and easy to use.

From Google Fonts, I picked Lexend and Quicksand. These are two sans serif fonts. That means these fonts do not have the strokes at the end of certain letterforms. I decided to use sans serif fonts as these are more modern and cleaner, making them easier to read.

I also wanted to keep the number of fonts used to a minimum, so the website would not look chaotic and the information I put across was easily accessible. For this reason, I decided to use Lexend for the headings. Lexend is a font family created by Dr. Bonnie Shaver-Troup and Google to improve reading-proficiency (Lexend.com). For the body of text, I chose Quicksand. This font was a project by Andrew Paglinawan in 2008. While it was designed with display purposes in mind, it was kept legible enough so it could be used in smaller sizes as well (Fonts.Google.com).

To maintain my vision and keep the website clean, I decided to establish the typographic hierarchy by using a different font on a larger size for the headings. Though this is one of the most common methods, it also provides simplicity.
